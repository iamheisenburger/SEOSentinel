## What Is Website Crawling for Keyword Research?

Website crawling is the automated process of scanning all pages on your website to gather data about content, structure, and keyword usage. A web crawler (bot) systematically navigates from one page to another, collecting information like titles, meta descriptions, headings, body text, internal links, and technical signals—everything search engines see when they index your site.[1]

For keyword research specifically, crawling creates a comprehensive inventory of every page, which keywords they target, how effectively those keywords are positioned, and where your site has content gaps. Instead of manually checking 50+ pages, a crawler does it in minutes.

**Why is this critical?** Because you can't optimize what you don't know exists. Crawling reveals:

- Which keywords your existing content already targets
- How well-optimized each page is for its target keyword
- Pages that rank but aren't optimized
- Gaps where competitors rank and you don't
- Technical issues preventing search engines from crawling and ranking your content

## Why Website Crawling Matters for SEO

Website crawling is the first step of any serious keyword research strategy because it grounds your research in reality—your actual website. Here's why it matters:

**1. Content Inventory & Keyword Mapping** — Crawling reveals which keywords you're already targeting and where they appear in your content. This prevents you from creating duplicate content and helps you understand your site's topical authority.[2] You'll quickly see if you have 5 pages about "content marketing" or none—which determines your next move.

**2. Identify Content Decay** — Pages that once ranked well often lose visibility over time as search intent evolves or competitors publish fresher content. Crawling paired with rank tracking reveals which articles are declining so you can refresh them with updated research and maintain traffic.[3] Without crawling, these drops go unnoticed for weeks.

**3. Uncover Content Gaps** — By analyzing what keywords your site targets versus what high-intent keywords exist in your niche, crawling reveals opportunities competitors are capturing. If your competitor ranks for "B2B SaaS onboarding best practices" and you don't have a page on that topic, crawling makes that gap visible.

**4. Assess Technical SEO** — Crawling detects technical issues like broken links, missing meta tags, slow page loads, and indexability problems that hurt keyword performance. A page with the perfect keyword but a broken internal linking structure won't rank as well as it should.

**5. Optimize Keyword Placement** — Crawling shows you how keywords are distributed across titles, headings, and body text. You might discover that your #1 traffic page targets a keyword in the body but never mentions it in the title—an easy optimization.

## Step 1: Choose Your Crawling Method

There are three main approaches to crawling: automated crawling tools, platform-specific crawlers, and built-in search console data. Here's how to pick the right one.

**Option A: Use a Dedicated SEO Crawler**

Tools like Screaming Frog, Crawlr, and similar platforms simulate how Google crawls your site. They generate detailed reports on every page, indexability signals, meta tags, headers, and link structure.[4]

*Best for:* Comprehensive technical audits and detailed keyword analysis. These tools work on any website size (though larger sites may require paid plans).

*How to start:* Download a crawler, point it at your domain, and let it run. It will map your entire site structure and generate a spreadsheet of pages with SEO metrics.

**Option B: Use Your CMS Built-in Tools**

WordPress plugins and other CMS platforms often include crawling features. Google Search Console also provides crawl data and shows how Google indexes your site.

*Best for:* Quick, free crawls without additional software. GSC data is especially useful for seeing which pages Google has indexed and how it's crawling your site.

*How to start:* Set up Google Search Console, link it to your site, and navigate to the Coverage report to see indexability status.

## Step 2: Run Your First Site Crawl

Running a site crawl is straightforward. Follow these steps to get your first complete inventory.

**1. Select Your Crawling Tool**

Choose one of Pentras mentioned above. For this guide, we'll assume you're using a standard SEO crawler (like Screaming Frog or a cloud-based equivalent).

**2. Configure Crawl Settings**

Before starting, set your crawl parameters:

- **Crawl Depth**: How many levels deep to crawl (e.g., crawl /blog and everything under it, or crawl the entire domain)
- **Crawl Speed**: How many requests per second. Slower is safer for production sites; faster is better for testing
- **Include/Exclude Patterns**: Exclude URL patterns you don't want indexed (e.g., /admin, /search results)
- **Follow Redirects**: Enable this so the crawler follows 301 redirects and reports final destination URLs

**3. Start the Crawl**

Point your crawler at your root domain (example.com) and let it run. For smaller sites (under 1,000 pages), this typically takes 5-30 minutes. Larger sites may take longer.

**4. Wait for Completion**

Once finished, the crawler will generate a report showing:

- Total pages crawled
- HTTP status codes (200, 404, 301, etc.)
- Meta titles and descriptions
- H1 and H2 tags
- Internal and external links
- Page load speed
- Mobile friendliness
- Indexability issues

**5. Export the Data**

Export the crawl report as a CSV or spreadsheet. This becomes your keyword research foundation—keep this file; you'll reference it throughout.

💡 **Pro Tip:** Run crawls monthly or quarterly to track changes. Compare crawls over time to spot new pages, deleted pages, and changes to existing content.

## Step 3: Analyze Existing Keywords

Now that you have a complete inventory of your site, it's time to map the keywords each page targets.

**1. Review Titles and Meta Descriptions**

Your crawl report includes the title tag and meta description for every page. These are SEO's most important real estate for keyword communication.

- **Action**: Go through the exported spreadsheet and identify the primary keyword each title targets. For example, a page with title "How to Write SEO Blog Posts: The Complete Guide" targets the keyword "how to write SEO blog posts."
- **Mark unclear titles**: If a page's title doesn't clearly indicate its keyword, flag it for later optimization.

**2. Analyze H1 and H2 Tags**

Your crawler should report the H1 (main heading) and first few H2s (subheadings) for each page. These tell you which keywords the content emphasizes.

- **Action**: Create a second column in your spreadsheet: "Primary Keyword from H1." Does the H1 keyword match the title keyword? If not, the page might be confusing search engines about what it's ranking for.
- **Example**: If the title says "SEO Blog Strategy" but the H1 says "Content Writing Tips," search engines might not associate the page with "SEO" as strongly as intended.

**3. Check Page Content and Keyword Density**

While your crawler report doesn't show every word on the page, you can manually review high-traffic or high-opportunity pages to assess keyword usage.

- **Action**: For your top 10-20 pages (by traffic or importance), open each in a browser and search (Ctrl+F) for the target keyword. Count how many times it appears.
- **Guideline**: Aim for 1-2% keyword density (if a page is 1,000 words, the keyword appears 10-20 times). More than 3% looks like keyword stuffing; less than 0.5% means you might not be emphasizing the keyword enough.

**4. Create a Keyword-to-Page Map**

Build a master spreadsheet like this:

| Page URL | Title Tag | Primary Keyword | H1 | Traffic (from GSC) | Status | --- |-----------|-----------|------------------|----|--------------------|----------| --- | /blog/seo-guide | How to Rank Higher: The SEO Guide | SEO guide | Best SEO Practices | 450/month | Optimized | --- | /blog/content-tips | 5 Content Writing Tips | content writing | Writing Basics | 120/month | Needs Refresh | --- | /about | About Our Company | company history | Who We Are | 0 | Not Optimized |

This map is your keyword inventory. It shows what you're currently targeting and what needs work.

💡 **Pro Tip:** Sync your crawl data with Google Search Console to overlay real search traffic and ranking data. This shows you which keywords are actually driving clicks.

## Step 4: Detect Content Gaps

Content gaps are keyword opportunities your site doesn't cover but your audience searches for and competitors rank for. Crawling reveals these gaps by showing what you *do* have; external research shows what you're *missing*.

**1. List Your Niche's High-Intent Keywords**

Use [keyword research tools](/#features) to generate a list of keywords relevant to your niche. If you sell B2B SaaS, search for keywords like:

- "B2B SaaS pricing models"
- "SaaS onboarding best practices"
- "SaaS customer retention strategy"
- "B2B software implementation"

Create a list of 50-100 high-intent keywords in your niche.

**2. Compare Against Your Keyword-to-Page Map**

Go through your keyword list and mark which ones you *already* have pages for. The keywords you don't have? Those are gaps.

**Example:**

| Keyword | Your Page | Competitor Page | Gap? | --- |---------|-----------|-----------------|------| --- | SaaS pricing models | ✗ No page | Ranking #3 | ✓ YES | --- | B2B onboarding | ✓ /blog/onboarding | Ranking #1 | ✗ No | --- | Customer retention SaaS | ✗ No page | Ranking #2 | ✓ YES |

**3. Prioritize High-Traffic Gaps**

Not all gaps are created equal. A gap with 50 monthly searches is less valuable than one with 1,000 monthly searches.

- **Action**: Use a keyword tool to estimate search volume for each gap. Prioritize gaps with:
  - High search volume (500+ monthly searches)
  - Medium-to-low difficulty (attackable by your site)
  - High commercial intent (leads/sales potential)

**4. Analyze Competitor Content**

If competitors rank for a keyword you don't cover, analyze their content:

- What's their angle? (Is it a how-to, comparison, listicle?)
- How long is the content? (Word count)
- What keywords do they target? (Beyond the primary keyword)
- Do they have unique data or primary research?

This informs how you'll address the gap when you create content.

💡 **Pro Tip:** Focus on "striking distance" keywords—those where you rank 11-20 or not at all but the search volume and competition suggest you *could* rank. These require less effort to move to the top 10 than new keywords.

## Step 5: Identify High-Opportunity Keywords

Not every content gap is worth pursuing. This step helps you identify which keywords will drive the most traffic and conversions.

**1. Assess Search Volume vs. Difficulty**

A keyword with 10,000 monthly searches sounds attractive, but if it has a keyword difficulty score of 85/100, you'll struggle to rank. Balance both:

- **Sweet spot**: 300-2,000 monthly searches + difficulty 20-50 = high opportunity
- **Quick wins**: 100-300 searches + difficulty <20 = fast ranking potential
- **Long-term plays**: 1,000+ searches + difficulty 50-70 = requires more effort, higher payoff

**2. Evaluate Commercial Intent**

Keywords fall into four intent categories:

- **Informational**: "How to write a blog post" (person researching, not buying)
- **Commercial Investigation**: "Best B2B SaaS tools" (comparing options)
- **Transactional**: "Buy SaaS template" (ready to purchase)
- **Navigational**: "Pentra login" (looking for a specific site)

For most businesses, commercial investigation and transactional keywords drive more conversions. Prioritize these.

**3. Estimate Traffic Potential**

Keyword rankings have diminishing returns. Position #1 gets 30% of clicks, #2 gets 15%, #3 gets 10%, and so on.[6] For a 1,000-search keyword:

- Rank #1 = ~300 monthly clicks
- Rank #3 = ~100 monthly clicks
- Rank #10 = ~20 monthly clicks

If you're new to a topic, targeting long-tail keywords (3-5 words) is easier. "Best SaaS" (2 words) is harder than "best SaaS for project management" (5 words).

**4. Create a Prioritized Opportunity List**

Build a ranking of gaps by opportunity:

| Gap Keyword | Volume | Difficulty | Intent | Traffic at #1 | Priority | --- |-------------|--------|------------|--------|---------------|----------| --- | SaaS customer retention | 850 | 35 | Commercial | 255 | HIGH | --- | B2B pricing strategy | 420 | 28 | Informational | 126 | MEDIUM | --- | SaaS implementation guide | 1,200 | 52 | Informational | 360 | MEDIUM | --- | Free SaaS templates | 150 | 18 | Transactional | 45 | LOW |

Start with HIGH priority gaps.

💡 **Pro Tip:** Use "keyword clustering" to group related keywords together. Instead of writing one article for "SaaS onboarding," you could write one comprehensive article targeting "SaaS onboarding," "SaaS implementation," and "new user activation"—three related keywords in one piece. This is how Pentra approaches keyword research automatically, clustering related keywords and planning content around them rather than treating each keyword in isolation.[7]

## Step 6: Plan Your Content Refresh

Once you've identified gaps and opportunities, it's time to create a content plan and refresh existing underperforming content.

**1. Create New Content for High-Priority Gaps**

For each high-priority gap, plan one article:

- **Topic**: The gap keyword
- **Angle**: How you'll differentiate from competitors (unique research, tool comparisons, case studies)
- **Format**: Blog post, guide, video, or case study
- **Target Keywords**: Primary gap keyword + 2-3 related keywords from your cluster
- **Publication Date**: Spread new content evenly (e.g., 1 article per week)

**2. Refresh Existing Underperforming Content**

Pages that rank 11-30 or have declining traffic are candidates for refresh:

- **Add fresh data**: Update statistics, add recent case studies
- **Expand coverage**: Add sections competitors have that you don't
- **Improve on-page SEO**: Strengthen title, H1, and keyword placement
- **Add internal links**: Link from new articles to this one, and from this one to related articles
- **Republish**: Update the publish date so it appears fresh in GSC

**3. Set Up Content Decay Monitoring**

Ranking positions change constantly. Set up monitoring to catch drops early:

- **Daily rank tracking**: Use your CMS, GSC, or a rank tracking tool to monitor your top 50 keywords daily
- **Alert threshold**: If a page drops 5+ positions, flag it for immediate refresh
- **Monthly review**: Review all pages to find slow declines (1-2 positions/month) that compound

**4. Build a Content Calendar**

Organize your content plan by month:

- **Month 1**: 4 new articles (gaps) + refresh 5 underperforming pages
- **Month 2**: 4 new articles + refresh 5 pages
- **Ongoing**: Monitor, detect decay, refresh one-off as needed

This gives you a repeatable system for continuous keyword optimization.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**1. Crawling Without a Plan**

Running a crawl and ignoring the results is useless. *Mistake:* Crawling your site once and never looking at the data again. *Fix:* Schedule crawls quarterly and always compare against previous crawls to spot trends.

**2. Targeting Keywords Without Search Volume**

*Mistake:* Focusing on keywords that sound relevant but have <50 monthly searches. *Fix:* Always verify search volume before creating content. 50-search keywords won't move the needle.

**3. Creating Content Without Addressing Existing Gaps First**

*Mistake:* Writing about new topics while leaving obvious gaps unfilled. *Fix:* Fill gaps in your existing niche first; it's easier to rank for keywords close to your authority.

**4. Forgetting Technical SEO During Crawl Analysis**

*Mistake:* Optimizing keywords but ignoring broken links, slow pages, or missing meta tags. *Fix:* Your crawl report flags technical issues—fix those first. A well-optimized page on a slow-loading site won't rank well.

**5. Not Refreshing Old Content**

*Mistake:* Writing 100 new articles and ignoring the 10 high-traffic articles that are slowly declining. *Fix:* Monitor your top pages constantly. Refresh them every 6-12 months with fresh data and examples.

**6. Underestimating Keyword Clustering**

*Mistake:* Writing separate articles for every related keyword variation instead of clustering them. *Fix:* Group related keywords (e.g., "SaaS onboarding," "SaaS implementation," "new user activation") and cover them all in one comprehensive article. This is far more efficient than creating three small articles.

## FAQ

### What does "crawlability" mean in SEO?

Crawlability refers to how easily search engines can access and navigate your website. High crawlability means search engines can discover all your pages, read your content, and understand your site structure. Low crawlability (e.g., blocked by robots.txt, slow pages, or broken internal links) prevents search engines from indexing your content fully. Website crawling tools test crawlability by simulating Google's crawler and reporting which pages can and can't be crawled.

### How often should I crawl my website?

For most sites, quarterly crawls (every 3 months) are sufficient to track major changes. If you publish frequently (10+ articles per month) or make significant site changes, monthly crawls help you stay on top of new content and technical issues. Use crawl comparisons to spot trends—if a new page isn't being crawled, for example, you'll know immediately rather than discovering it months later.[9]

### Can I crawl a competitor's website to find keyword gaps?

No—crawling your own site and analyzing competitor sites are different processes. You can crawl your own domain to understand what *you* target. To analyze competitors, use keyword research tools and manual site inspection to see which keywords they rank for and what content they've created. Crawling your own site reveals your gaps; researching competitors reveals what topics matter to your audience.

### What's the difference between crawling and indexing?

Crawling is the process of visiting and reading your pages (what crawling tools and Google's crawler do). Indexing is the process of storing and organizing that information in a search engine's database. Your pages can be crawled but not indexed (if you've blocked them with noindex), or they might not be crawled at all (if they're blocked by robots.txt). Crawl your own site to monitor crawlability; use Google Search Console to monitor indexation.

### Do I need to hire an SEO expert to do website crawling?

No. Website crawling tools are designed for non-technical users. Most modern crawlers have simple interfaces: enter your domain, click "start crawl," and download a report. The harder part is *interpreting* the data and taking action on it. If you follow the steps in this guide, you can crawl and analyze your site yourself. For ongoing keyword research and content planning, automation platforms like Pentra handle the interpretation and execution automatically.

### How does website crawling improve keyword rankings?

Crawling itself doesn't improve rankings—but the *action* you take based on crawl data does. By crawling, you identify underperforming pages, technical issues, and content gaps. You then refresh underperforming pages with fresh research, fix technical issues, and create content for gaps. These actions improve rankings. Without crawling, you're flying blind and wasting time creating content you don't need or missing opportunities.

### What should I do if my crawl shows a lot of broken links?

Broken internal links hurt user experience and waste crawl budget (the number of pages Google crawls on your site per day). When you find broken links in your crawl report: (1) Identify the pages with broken links, (2) Fix them by redirecting to a working page or updating the link, (3) Re-crawl to verify. Fixing broken links often results in immediate ranking improvements because you're improving technical SEO.

### Can crawling help me find long-tail keyword opportunities?

Indirectly, yes. Your crawl shows you which keywords you're *already* targeting. By looking at existing long-tail keyword content, you can brainstorm related long-tail variations. For example, if you have content for "B2B SaaS pricing," related long-tails include "B2B SaaS pricing models," "B2B SaaS pricing strategy," and "B2B SaaS pricing tiers." However, comprehensive long-tail research requires external keyword tools. Crawling provides context; keyword research tools provide discovery.
